Results & Effectiveness

Will tutoring actually improve my child's grades?

+

Yes. Our track record speaks for itself: in 2025, our students achieved a median of 96 in Latin Continuers and 48 in Latin Extension, with a median ATAR of 99.6.

We delivered 12.5% of all top band results in NSW for both Latin Continuers and Extension. Over the past 8 years, our students have received 30+ first-in-grade awards across Sydney's top schools including James Ruse, Pymble Ladies, and Hills Grammar.

Our small group model allows tutors to identify each student's weaknesses, consolidate fundamentals, and provide targeted practice that directly translates to better marks.

How long does it take to see improvement?

+

Most students see noticeable improvements within 4-6 weeks, with significant grade improvements typically appearing by the end of the first term.

However, this depends on where your child starts. Students who are keeping up but want to excel may see results faster, while those who need to catch up on fundamentals may take a bit longer to build confidence and see marks improve.

The key is consistency—regular attendance and practice between sessions accelerate progress.

Is my child too far behind to catch up?

+

No. We've successfully helped students at all levels, from those struggling with basics to those aiming for state rankings.

Our tutors are skilled at identifying gaps in understanding and systematically filling them. We focus on building strong fundamentals first—grammar rules, vocabulary strategies, and translation techniques—before moving to more advanced work.

Many of our highest-achieving students started with us needing significant support. With the right guidance and consistent effort, catching up is absolutely possible.

Class Structure & Learning

How many students are in each class?

+

Our classes have 8-12 students—small enough for personalized attention, large enough for peer learning and discussion.

This size allows tutors to:

  • Notice when individual students are struggling
  • Provide targeted feedback on translations and grammar
  • Foster collaborative learning where students help each other
  • Create a supportive environment where asking questions feels safe

Students report feeling much more comfortable asking questions in our small groups than in large classrooms or intimidating one-on-one settings.

How do you handle different ability levels in one class?

+

We group students by their current Latin level (Latin I, II, III, Extension) to ensure everyone is working on appropriate material.

Within each class, our tutors use differentiated instruction—giving extension work to faster students while providing extra support to those who need it. The small group size makes this individualization possible.

Students also learn from each other. Sometimes hearing a concept explained by a peer makes it click in a way teacher explanations don't.

Will my child feel comfortable asking questions?

+

Yes. Creating a safe, encouraging environment is one of our core priorities.

Many students feel embarrassed asking questions in their school classes. Our small groups foster a supportive atmosphere where:

  • Everyone is there because they want to improve—there's no judgment
  • Tutors actively encourage questions and normalize mistakes as part of learning
  • Students realize their classmates have similar struggles
  • The pace is flexible—we slow down if a concept needs more explanation

Building confidence is just as important as building knowledge.

What happens during a typical session?

+

Each 90-minute session typically includes:

  • Grammar review: Clarifying and consolidating key concepts (cases, conjugations, syntax)
  • Translation practice: Working through passages together with guided support
  • Vocabulary strategies: Effective techniques for retention and recall
  • Question time: Addressing specific student challenges and homework questions
  • Exam preparation: Practice with past papers and HSC-style questions

Sessions are interactive, not lecture-based. Students actively participate, translate, and problem-solve together.

+

While regular attendance is crucial for progress, we provide session recordings so students can catch up on anything they missed. For extended absences, we can recommend additional resources or practice materials to keep students on track.

How much time commitment is required outside of class?

+

We recommend 1-2 hours of practice per week outside of class sessions. This includes:

  • Vocabulary review (15-20 minutes, several times per week)
  • Practice translations
  • Grammar exercises
  • Reviewing session materials

Latin is cumulative—consistent, regular practice is far more effective than cramming. Even 20 minutes of daily vocabulary review makes a huge difference.

When should we start tutoring?

+

The best time to start is as soon as you notice your child struggling—or even before that if you want them to get ahead.

Many of our most successful students started early (Year 9 or 10) and built strong foundations from the beginning. However, we've also helped Year 12 students make significant improvements in their final year.

Starting earlier gives more time to consolidate fundamentals, but it's never too late to improve.

Latin-Specific Questions

My child finds Latin grammar overwhelming. Can you help?

+

Absolutely. Latin grammar—declensions, conjugations, cases—is complex, but it becomes manageable when broken down systematically.

Our tutors excel at making grammar accessible. They:

  • Explain concepts in multiple ways until they click
  • Use patterns and memory techniques to simplify learning
  • Provide plenty of practice with immediate feedback
  • Connect grammar to translation so students see why it matters

Many students who initially found Latin impossible discover they actually enjoy it once the fundamentals make sense.

How do you help with vocabulary retention?

+

Vocabulary is one of the biggest challenges in Latin. We teach proven strategies:

  • Etymology: Connecting Latin words to English derivatives
  • Word families: Learning related words together
  • Spaced repetition: Regular review schedules for long-term retention
  • Context: Learning words through reading, not just lists

We also recommend effective tools and techniques students can use independently.

Will this help with translation or just grammar?

+

Both! Grammar is the foundation, but translation is the goal.

We spend significant time on translation practice—teaching students how to:

  • Identify sentence structure and key elements
  • Make informed guesses when unsure
  • Produce fluent, natural English translations
  • Work efficiently under exam conditions

Translation is a skill that improves with practice and feedback, both of which we provide in abundance.
